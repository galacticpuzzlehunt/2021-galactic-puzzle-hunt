‚Æ¨Tips to reading Bel

Great, we’ve got a handle on our goal now. We can figure out each language on pages 23‚Äì30 based on the sounds we identify and by clues in the image. If we learn to read enough of Bel, we can read the mysterious phrase on page 31, which will tell us what to do next. There are many ways forward now, so here are some potential discoveries or hints to make progress:

  1. Bel is a phonetic script, since it needs to represent many languages, not all of which are written in Latin. Examples include Arabic ŸÇŸáŸàÿ©, Mandarin Chinese ÂíñÂï°, and Cantonese (page 30).

  2. Consonants are based off the bell shape on the left-most torn page, with three degrees of features:

    a) voiced or unvoiced

    b) place of articulation

    c) manner of articulation

  3. Vowels are based off the curved strokes, as seen extending off the bell on the left-most torn page, with three degrees of features:

    a) rounded or unrounded

    b) vowel height

    c) vowel backness

  4. Tone markers are loopy curves that extend from vowel curves, which can be used to help identify the tonal languages Mandarin Chinese (page 22), Vietnamese (page 24), and Cantonese (page 30).

  5. The direction of curvature changes along word boundaries.

  6. Each language page (pages 23‚Äì30) features a particular language and a particular food item from the language speakers’ cuisine. To be precise, each page has the name of the language, and a dialogue between two humans in that language, with puflantu translations. The offscreen human says: “Hello! What is this?” and the pictured human responds, “This is” followed by the name of the food they are showing.

Once we have enough of these observations, we can start reading page 31. It’s a long puflantu sentence, with four non-puflantu words, each followed by the same short puflantu suffix each time. What were your inspirations for this puzzle?

This puzzle is my loving homage to the wonderful games Outer Wilds and Tunic, both of which blend narrative with puzzle-solving in startlingly unique ways. If you haven’t played either of these games, you’re in for a treat!

(Outer Wilds spoilers) Of course, our hunt structure was based on time loops, and many of us were inspired by our love for Outer Wilds. The curve aesthetic of Bel was directly influenced by the non-linear spirals of Nomai script. This puzzle is actually an Outer Wilds prequel where you play as Hal and make the Nomai translator tool!

(Tunic spoilers) The core mechanic of this puzzle, as well as the flipbook styling, is straight from the manual of Tunic, which is written in an English phonetic script named Runic. Learning to read Runic was an experience I wanted to give more people, and I saw a unique opportunity to do something similar (with a galactic twist) here.

Ooh so curvy! What were some of the design decisions when creating Bel?
  • Be rotational/reflection invariant ‚Äî imagine a big crowd of aliens gathered around in space around a holographic projection, and they can read it from any angle... like this:
  • Don’t be dependent on sharp/smooth distinction ‚Äî meaning it can be handwritten quickly and easily read (doctors hate it!)

Why include tones?

My initial design for Bel was for tones to be a “first-class citizen” in the script. Unfortunately, they’re not so much the case in the end product (they don’t even feature in the extraction, and were almost cut from the puzzle entirely!) In fact, the tone markers play a double role in both notating vowel pitch (or “tone”) as well as variations on alveolar consonants. The system of tones labeling alveolar, palatal, post-alveolar, and retroflex consonants is based on the extremely niche fact that if you say /s…ï É Ç/, the frequency of the sound decreases!

Why does the vowel chart look different from the IPA chart and omit some vowels?
Vowels are already an abstract discretization of a continuous space, in reality characterised by acoustic formants more than by tongue height or backness. Some of IPA’s vowels, like /…™/, are rooted in a kind of historic Eurocentrism. I picked the nearest vowels that fit the design best, and hope you don’t mind re-quantising your vowels a little. (You know what else is arbitrary in Canada? Spelling words with s or z.)
Why are there no uvulars in Bel?
Aliens don’t have uvulas. Uvulae? Actually, they don’t have teeth either, which is why their f and v are bilabial instead of labiodental, but they respect human teeth.
What about suprasegmentals/punctuation/intonation/aspiration?
Feel free to make your own extensions. You could make the vowel lines longer for long vowels. As for intonation, you could potentially reproduce this with a combination of tone markers, vowel reduction, and vowel length. Aspiration? There’s no air in space!
