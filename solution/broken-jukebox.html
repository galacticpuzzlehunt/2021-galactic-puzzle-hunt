Solution Broken Jukebox
Answer DEATH

Written by CJ Quines and Mark

Looking at all the lights together, we notice that groups of them appear to be flashing in sync. Covering all lights except for those in each group, we observe that one of these groups encodes a message in pigpen, while another encodes a message in semaphores. Covering those lights allows us to discover two more such groups, one encoding a message in binary and the other in braille.

At this point, we may decode the messages we’ve found to find the message TWO LIGHTS PER SONG (encoded in semaphores), as well as three crossword clues for well-known meme songs. We also notice that many of the remaining lights flash at a steady beat. Upon further inspection, we find that each song is associated with two lights. One flashes to the rhythm of the song’s melody, while the other flashes in sync with the song’s beats. In fact, each such pair of lights belong to the same column, with the melody above and the beats below. Only a short but recognizable snippet of each song is represented, looped over and over across the duration of the puzzle.

Looking at the remaining lights, we find two more crossword clues for meme songs, as well as the messages INTERFERE PAIRS OF LIGHTS FROM XKCD 1331 (encoded in Morse) and TO GET BEAT FREQS IN BPM BE PRECISE (encoded in unary, i.e., 1 blink for “A”, 2 blinks for “B”, 3 blinks for “C”, etc.). The relative positions of the two messages in the grid, along with the fact that the second message starts with “TO”, suggests that they combine to form an instruction for what to do next.

We refer to the Explain xkcd page for xkcd comic 1331: Frequency to find the exact periods of each of the comic’s GIFs. In order to achieve the level of precision suggested by the “beats” values at the bottom of the puzzle, we make sure to use the periods reported in Explain xkcd rather than the frequencies. As the page explains, the periods were computed directly from the GIFs’ metadata, which specifies animation frame lengths in hundredths of a second. The periods are therefore exact.

We now compute the beat frequencies that would result from “interfering” the GIFs in the same positions as each song’s light pair. To do so, we invert their periods to get frequencies, convert them to beats per minute, then compute the difference between each pair. We then match the decimal portions of the beat frequencies with the “beats” values at the bottom of the puzzle and apply the corresponding shifts to the integral portions. This gives us a number associated with each song.

Arranging these numbers in the given order of the “beats” values puts them in ascending order, suggesting that a different order is required. Instead, we use the order of the columns that each song’s lights appear in. Indexing the numbers into the song titles then gives us the answer, DEATH.

Author’s Notes

CJ: The working name for this puzzle was “Blinkenlights”. Its Wikipedia page has one of my favorite computer-related quotes, “please keep still and only watchen astaunished the blinkenlights.” I wanted a puzzle that could replicate the feeling of looking at a bunch of lights blinking together. And a grid of blinking lights reminded me of the xkcd comic, so that’s how the extraction came along.

Given the answer was DEATH, the original title was “Broken AED”, and we chose different songs. We considered Another One Bites the Dust, Stayin’ Alive, I Will Survive, Tearin’ Up My Heart, Achy Breaky Heart, The Sound of Silence, and Highway to Hell, the connection being that these were all songs you can do CPR to. But they didn’t have rhythms or melodies that you could recognize just by looking at blinking lights, so we had to scrap that idea. (I thought it’d be funny, though.)
