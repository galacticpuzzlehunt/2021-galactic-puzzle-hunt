Solution Maze
Answer NUTS

Written by Jon Schneider

This puzzle contains 4 separate “mazes” connected by red hallways. Walking around these mazes, we might notice that something about these mazes feels weird—sometimes it feels like we have returned to a location we were in previously, only to see a different pattern of walls. In fact, all of the 4 mazes are self-overlapping (and hence physically impossible to construct).

Each of the 4 mazes can be thought of as a 5-by-5 grid containing some number of rooms (maps for each of the 4 grids are available below). Each cell in this grid could possibly contain more than one room, and in each cardinal direction, each room either has a wall or a connection to some other room (in the appropriate spot of the 5-by-5 grid). For example, in the first maze, starting from room 12 and proceeding west, north, then east, we walk through rooms 31, 28, and 29 (which is in the same absolute location as room 7).

Notes on reading this map: each room is assigned a unique number. Twenty-five of the rooms (labeled 1‚Äì25, and always arranged the same way in the 5-by-5 grid) are chosen to be “base rooms”, and some more rooms (forming the extracted letter, labeled with numbers after 25) are chosen to be “extra rooms”. In certain places on the map, it is possible to walk between a base room and an extra room. Those transitions are indicated with red walls in both the upper/lower floor plans (and the lower floor plan makes it clear which base rooms are connected to which extra rooms).

If we highlight the cells of this 5-by-5 grid where two different rooms are present (the overlaps of the maze with itself), this spells out the answer NUTS.

Alternate explanation (courtesy of Ben Yang): Here is another way you can think about this puzzle and its extraction. Pretend there are two people: one in the maze in the puzzle, and one in an empty 5-by-5 grid of the same size with no walls. Everytime the person in the puzzle maze takes a step in a direction, so does the person in the empty grid. In addition, every time the person in the puzzle maze finds themselves in a cell they haven’t visited yet, they draw an X on the ground, and so does the person in the empty room (regardless of whether there’s already an X on the floor in the empty room).

Now, after the person in the puzzle maze has fully explored the puzzle maze (placed an X in every reachable room), each of the cells in the empty 5-by-5 grid will have either 1 or 2 Xs on the ground. The cells with 2 X marks are the “overlaps”, and they form letters that extract to the solution.
