Solution Somewhere
Answer OVERFLIGHT

Written by Alan Huang

We start by trying to match directions to starting points. Finding the right combinations can be tricky, but the matches are intended to be fairly self-confirming. We can also work backward by for example trying to think of well-known US places that are famous for races or universities, although this is likely to be harder.

There are fifteen sets of directions, so we may imagine that three of them correspond to each starting point. However, some are implausible ‚Äî there is no capital on a northern coast we can reasonably get to, for instance, and deserts and isthmuses are also in short supply in eastern North America. Furthermore, the row of ten blanks at the bottom suggests we may want a set of ten of some kind. We can in fact make ten matches, listed below in puzzle order:

We should notice the following facts that can help us with the matching:

  • A “major road” here always refers to an interstate highway.
  • If the clue reads “head [direction]”, that means as the crow flies, not on a road.
  • The destination places all have two-word names, and are in alphabetical order.

This last point is interesting since we also have two destinations per starting point. Combining these produces phrases that sound like plausible place names ‚Äî and they are, only in fictional universes instead of the real world.

Now we can look at the numbers associated with each set of directions. If we take the real and fictional locations separately, we notice that the latter have the numbers 1, 2, 3, 4, 5. Therefore, these provide an ordering for the starting locations. The other numbers look like indices, but what do we index into? We’ve already used the real destinations to create the fictional starting points, but we haven’t used the fictional destinations yet.

Indexing by the two real-world numbers associated with each fictional destination gives us the desired ten letters, OVERFLIGHT.
