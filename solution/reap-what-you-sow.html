Solution Reap What You Sow
Answer LOAM

Written by Anderson Wang
Idea by Alan Huang

This is a puzzle about mancala. While there are many variants of this game, they all center around sowing, the act of picking up all the pieces in a hole and dropping one in each subsequent hole going around the board. This game is played between two players, one using the bottom holes and right bank, the other using the top holes and left bank. Sowing proceeds counterclockwise and skips the opponent’s bank. There is no capturing, but if a player finishes a sow on their bank, they move again.

We should begin by solving some of the easier clues. Some answers on nearby turns use similar letters, and we can start assembling these into chains. This lets us solidify our understanding of the game and disambiguate any rules questions. Each seed in a hole is a letter; as indicated by the diagram, each hole starts with four. On a turn, the underlined clue gives the sown word and the other clues give the words formed in other holes by distributing its letters. Which letter goes in which hole must be deduced. A series of dots indicates that the player’s bank now has that many seeds in it.

Once we finish playing out the given turns, we can read the letters that went into each bank in order: ENDINA TURN.

It is currently the first player’s turn (because they just finished the ASA move in their bank), and in most versions of mancala, the game ends when there are no seeds left on one side of the board. So, this cluephrase implies we need to clear the seeds from the first player’s side in one turn, by repeatedly making moves that end in the bank. There is a unique order of moves that clears all the seeds—in particular, whenever there are multiple possible moves that end in the bank, we have to pick the one closest to the bank (since otherwise, that hole will get an extra seed, and then any move starting from that hole can no longer end in the bank).

Additionally, we can see that the bottom set of answers correspond to the words picked up on each move, which lets us disambiguate which seed goes in which hole. For example, on the fourth move, we pick up the word AMIDST from the leftmost hole, and then the letter I must go in the CYIL hole to make ICILY, the letter A must go in the LX hole to eventually make ALYX, etc. The full sequence of moves is as follows:

Author’s Notes

Anderson: The rules used in this game are basically the same as in Kalah, which is the version I remember playing in middle school. The final extraction was inspired by the interesting fact that for any number n, there is exactly one position with n seeds that can be cleared in one turn.

To construct the mancala game, I wrote a program to search for sequences of moves that would result in a seed pattern that could be cleared in one turn, among other constraints. Then, I used a big spreadsheet to keep track of individual seeds and the words they were part of, and judiciously used Nutrimatic to search for good chains of transadditions. Luckily, anagramming made it flexible enough to get pretty reasonable words by hand (YLIC is the worst offender but was basically required for the final extraction), though I did write a quick script to find the long chain from GLARE to ORGANELLE.

Alan: There are a lot of classic board games out there that are popular in their own right but haven’t gotten their chance to shine as a puzzle!
