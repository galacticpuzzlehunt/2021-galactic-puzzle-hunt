The Obligatory Interactive Puzzle

This puzzle has two sections, delineated by a horizontal dividing line. The puzzle starts out with some buttons (‚ÄúWait‚Äù and ‚ÄúClick me‚Äù). If you click on the buttons, checkboxes with lists of numbers next to them will appear. Once a box is checked, it cannot be un-checked.

We now wonder how these buttons and checkboxes relate to previous Galactic Puzzle Hunts. The ‚ÄúClick me‚Äù button increments a counter, and when the counter passes certain thresholds, more checkboxes appear. This may remind us of the 2018 Galactic Puzzle Hunt, in which obtaining a certain number of cookies (through clicking on cookie buttons and solving puzzles) would unlock more puzzles.

The first section corresponds to GPH 2017, which unlocked 5 puzzles every day. In this section, only the ‚ÄúWait‚Äù button creates more checkboxes (checking checkboxes does nothing).

Each checkbox represents one puzzle. The checkboxes appear in the same order as on the GPH 2017 and GPH 2018 websites.

Now we have to figure out what to do with the numbers. It turns out each number corresponds to a letter, and the number will appear in the list next to a checkbox iff the puzzle title contains the corresponding letter. By comparing puzzle titles and numbers, we can determine the following:

Author’s Notes

Overall structure commentary

Lewis: We were somewhat set on using the rules conditions (internally known as the ‚Äúlong con‚Äù, since it was over five years in the making) this year. None of our metas could take on the answer QUINAPALUS (and it could cause balance issues for teams that had presolved this ahead of time) so instead this became a subpuzzle answer. (Meta spoilers) One of the puzzles was required by the meta structure to be a minipuzzle set with five subpuzzles, so this became that puzzle. It was certainly an interesting exercise to construct such a set of five subpuzzles, with an answer that was eight letters long, and with a fixed feeder. I noticed that there was a UP in QUINAPALUS, which motivated the adjacent shared bigram mechanic.

Later on, I noticed that ‚Äúthe number of years referenced‚Äù could serve as a sort of ordering mechanism when we had written puzzles 3, 4, and 5. (These are complete sets, because when the 2022 hunt was running, teams clearly did not have access to the Spotify playlist or funny answer submissions for that year, and we apparently also didn‚Äôt compile funny answer submissions in 2019). So the remaining two puzzles also fit this pattern (it was noticed that we wrote a total of two unlocking documents in 2018 and 2019, but ultimately we didn‚Äôt pursue this direction).

Also, testsolving this puzzle is somewhat tricky given that our own team is understandably a less-than-representative sample of people who would know about various details of our hunt. We were lucky to have some outside testsolvers, and we also tried to ensure that the GPH referencing was not too much of a leap with things like the meta diagram.

Commentary for each of the subpuzzles as follows:

Rays of Sunlight

Yes, the leaderboard changed over time over the course of the hunt and so does this subpuzzle. We wrote about 600 clues to try to ensure that we can always find 10 teams in leaderboard order.

Brute Force

The long con that started it all. Initially we put the remark in 2017‚Äôs rules page without too much thought as to what the ‚Äúfinal answer‚Äù would be. By 2018 we had a better idea and so set up a plan to continue to narrow down possibilities for the final answer to this subpuzzle. We sort of forgot about actually having one of our metas resolve to QUINAPALUS, although this is probably for the best since people move in and out of our writing team over the years (and several teams outside have picked up on this as well), so making this only a fifth of the whole puzzle was good to relatively minimize the amount of advantage for teams that already know of the answer.
